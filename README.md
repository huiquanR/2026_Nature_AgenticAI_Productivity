## AI agents in research: when productivity comes at the cost of apprenticeship

### By Jing Hu & Tony Huiquan Zhang

*******

Download PDF
In the past few months, we have worked extensively with the artificial-intelligence assistants Claude Code and OpenClaw on various research projects. They have been the perfect assistants that any researcher would want: they are always available, can review literature, grasp our guidance quickly and can debug complex codes in minutes or hours, rather than weeks or months. It’s tempting to outsource most data collection, cleaning and curation to these tools. We doubt we are alone in feeling this way.

But we have noticed something that troubles us. We have not assigned substantive tasks to our graduate research assistants in weeks. We still advise them on their papers, but when it comes to our own research agenda, we have quietly turned to AI agents instead. As a result, our meetings with members of our research groups have become less frequent, and students are less involved in our projects’ research procedures. They know less than they might otherwise have done about the choices, dead ends and small calibrations that shape a project and lead to a product.

That realization gave us a jolt. AI saves time, but it also deprives students of the cognitive scaffolding they need to become autonomous scholars. As mentors to emerging scholars, we must balance the use of AI with the need to preserve the hands-on research experiences required for students’ growth.
